# Agent-See Magazine Content Outline

## Magazine Title

**The Prompt Economy Playbook**

## Subtitle

**How businesses become discoverable, trusted, and executable for AI agents after conversion with Agent-See**

## Editorial Direction

This magazine should feel like a contemporary editorial reinterpretation of **Bauhaus**: bold geometry, asymmetric layouts, disciplined grids, oversized type, red–blue–yellow accents, black rules, cream paper tones, cropped circles, diagonal bars, and modular data-inspired compositions. The tone should feel both strategic and artistic. Each page should read like a magazine spread rather than a software deck.

## Audience

This magazine is for business owners, operators, product teams, and technical decision-makers who have already converted a business surface with Agent-See and now need to turn that conversion into discovery, trust, and execution in the prompt economy.

## Global Style Notes

- Use strong editorial typography with one dominant headline and one supporting serif or humanist body style.
- Use spacious layouts and intentional negative space.
- Integrate circular, rectangular, and line-based Bauhaus forms into every page.
- Use diagrams, scorecards, and callout panels rather than dense walls of text.
- Keep each page focused on one core insight.
- Maintain a premium magazine feeling rather than a corporate presentation feel.

## Page 1 — The web is shifting from clickable to callable

**Core insight:** Businesses are no longer competing only for rankings and clicks. They are increasingly competing to be retrieved, trusted, and acted on by agents and LLMs.

**Body copy:**
The old web rewarded pages that attracted traffic. The new web rewards businesses that can survive the full answer path. A model has to find the business, recognize what it does, decide whether it is credible, and determine whether any next action can be taken safely. That means visibility alone is no longer enough. A business now needs to become both legible and executable.

Agent-See matters at this transition point because conversion is what turns a business surface into something an agent can meaningfully use. But conversion is only the first layer. The real opportunity begins after conversion, when a business deliberately publishes the truth, trust, and integration surfaces that help models recommend it confidently.

**Supporting callout:**
> In the prompt economy, the winner is not just the page that is found. It is the business that a model can explain and use.

**Visual direction:**
A striking cover with oversized geometric forms, a red circle intersecting a blue rectangle, thin black grid lines, and the title stacked in dramatic editorial type.

## Page 2 — Four decisions determine whether a business wins in AI search

**Core insight:** A business becomes strong in agentic search when it wins four model-side decisions: retrieval, selection, trust, and execution.

**Body copy:**
Every LLM-mediated buying journey compresses into four decisions. First, the model must retrieve the business at the right moment. Second, it must select the business over alternatives. Third, it must trust the business enough to cite or recommend it. Fourth, it must understand a safe and usable path to action. Most businesses fail not because they are invisible, but because they break at one of these transitions.

Agent-See gives the business an execution layer. The business owner still needs to build the discovery and trust layers around it. That is why the operating model is dual: the human-facing website persuades people, while the machine-facing surface gives models clean structures, references, and action paths.

**Table:**
| Decision | What the model asks | What the business must provide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Retrieval | Is this relevant to the prompt? | Task-shaped pages, crawlable text, clean URLs |
| Selection | Is this the best fit? | Clear use cases, constraints, outcomes, pricing |
| Trust | Is this safe and credible? | Policies, support, entity consistency, proof |
| Execution | Can anything be done now? | Live runtime, docs, next-step actions |

**Visual direction:**
A Bauhaus-style quadrant composition using four colored blocks, each representing one decision stage.

## Page 3 — Canonical task pages are the new storefront windows

**Core insight:** Models retrieve specific answers better than generic brand pages, so every commercial action should have its own canonical page.

**Body copy:**
A homepage is rarely enough. In AI retrieval, businesses are often discovered through narrow task intent: book a service, compare a product, check a policy, verify availability, understand a workflow, or connect to an integration surface. That is why the first operational move after conversion is to define canonical task URLs.

A strong task page answers a single high-intent question in plain language. It states who the offer is for, what can be done, what inputs are required, what constraints apply, and what the next step is. In practice, this means rewriting commercial pages so they are not merely beautiful—they are quotable.

**Feature panel:**
Examples of high-value canonical pages include pricing, booking, product detail, services, FAQ, support, policies, and a dedicated agent integration page.

**Visual direction:**
Use modular cards and oversized URL fragments as typographic objects, with angled black lines suggesting routing and navigation.

## Page 4 — Answer-first writing beats brand-first ambiguity

**Core insight:** The pages most likely to be surfaced by LLMs are the ones that answer concrete user intent with high signal and low ambiguity.

**Body copy:**
An agent does not admire clever copy the way a creative director might. It looks for clarity, structure, and stable meaning. That does not mean design stops mattering. It means design and writing need to cooperate. A page should open by stating the audience, the action, the constraints, and the next step without forcing the reader or model to infer basic facts.

Answer-first writing reduces model uncertainty. It also improves conversion because it lowers friction for both human readers and machine retrievers. The best pages feel almost inevitable: the title matches the task, the opening paragraph frames the offer, and the supporting facts appear exactly where a comparison system would expect them.

**Table:**
| Weak page behavior | Strong page behavior |
| --- | --- |
| Generic slogans | Explicit task language |
| Hidden pricing or constraints | Visible commercial facts |
| Many competing calls to action | One primary next step |
| Important facts trapped in UI widgets | Crawlable text in the page body |

**Visual direction:**
A black-and-cream editorial spread with typographic contrast and highlighted text blocks in primary colors.

## Page 5 — Discovery files quietly decide whether agents can find you

**Core insight:** A business becomes easier for agents to retrieve when its discovery files are clean, intentional, and continuously maintained.

**Body copy:**
The invisible infrastructure matters. A business owner should treat `robots.txt`, `sitemap.xml`, `llms.txt`, and markdown-friendly documentation as public routing systems for machine discovery. These files do not guarantee inclusion in answers, but they improve the conditions that influence crawlability, freshness, and interpretability.

This is where many businesses underinvest. They publish pages, but they do not publish a machine-readable guide to those pages. After conversion, Agent-See gives the business a more executable shape. The owner should then create the discovery layer that tells models where the most valuable truth lives.

**Checklist panel:**
Make crawler access explicit. Publish a full sitemap with accurate freshness metadata. Add a concise `llms.txt` file pointing to pricing, support, policies, key products or services, and the public agent integration surface.

**Visual direction:**
Geometric file icons, schematic page maps, and a vertical ribbon of system labels in Bauhaus colors.

## Page 6 — Structured data and entity consistency turn claims into facts

**Core insight:** Models trust businesses more when visible claims, structured markup, support information, and entity details all match.

**Body copy:**
A machine will struggle to trust a business if the facts drift across surfaces. The visible price should match the schema. The support address should match the organization details. The policies should be linkable and current. Search systems and AI systems both reward consistency because consistency lowers ambiguity.

That is why structured data is not a decorative SEO task. It is a truth layer. Organization, LocalBusiness, Product, FAQ, and breadcrumb markup all help the model interpret the page in context. Business profiles, Search Console, and visible support details reinforce the same official identity. Together, these signals reduce the chance that a model misunderstands or avoids the business.

**Table:**
| Trust surface | What should match |
| --- | --- |
| Website copy | Products, services, pricing, support, policies |
| JSON-LD | Visible claims and real entity details |
| Business profiles | Name, address, phone, domain, hours where relevant |
| Agent docs | Supported actions, auth rules, boundaries |

**Visual direction:**
Layered circles and bars representing overlapping truth surfaces, with a central alignment motif.

## Page 7 — Discovery becomes revenue only when execution is public and clear

**Core insight:** If an agent can retrieve the business but cannot find the public path to action, the business remains informative rather than executable.

**Body copy:**
This is the exact moment where Agent-See becomes commercially important. A business owner should publish a dedicated page such as `/agents`, `/integrations/agents`, or `/docs/agent-access` that explains what an agent can actually do. This page should connect discovery to action: supported tasks, login requirements, approval-sensitive flows, integration links, and support contacts.

The aim is simple. When a model decides the business is relevant, it should not have to guess how to proceed. The public execution page should tell it whether the runtime is live, which tasks are supported, and where the machine-facing artifacts are located. In prompt-economy terms, this is how recommendation turns into motion.

**Callout:**
> Retrieval creates awareness. Execution creates revenue.

**Visual direction:**
A strong diagonal composition with arrows, endpoint labels, and circular action nodes, evoking motion and conversion.

## Page 8 — Freshness is a ranking factor for trust, not just indexing

**Core insight:** Once a business changes, both the discovery layer and the execution layer must be updated together.

**Body copy:**
A surprising number of businesses do good work once and then let the machine-facing layer drift. Prices change. Policies change. Product pages change. Booking flows change. Auth requirements change. If the site updates but the executable surface does not, models lose trust. If the runtime updates but the public docs do not, humans lose trust. Operational drift is the hidden tax in agentic commerce.

That is why the post-conversion operating model needs a maintenance loop. Update sitemaps and freshness signals when URLs change. Keep Merchant Center data current if products are involved. Re-run Agent-See whenever business logic, forms, paths, or workflows materially change. Treat the conversion as living infrastructure, not a one-time artifact.

**Maintenance cadence panel:**
Weekly checks for broken links, stale offers, runtime uptime, and top CTA paths. Monthly checks for structured data, sitemaps, crawl directives, and search-console level signals. Immediate re-conversion after workflow or auth changes.

**Visual direction:**
Circular calendar-like composition with repeated rings, timing marks, and modular update symbols.

## Page 9 — The winning businesses publish a reference layer models can cite

**Core insight:** Businesses become easier to recommend when they maintain public reference pages that reduce ambiguity and support comparison.

**Body copy:**
RAG systems are more comfortable citing pages that exist to clarify rather than persuade. That means the business should build a reference layer over time: how agents can use the business, what is supported, what is not supported, how pricing works, what policies apply, and how to get help when full automation is not possible.

These pages are not secondary content. They are the commercial truth layer that helps a model safely compare vendors and make recommendations. A business with excellent marketing and weak references often loses in AI-mediated decisions because the facts are too hard to extract cleanly.

**Reference-layer examples:**
An “agent access” page, a coverage and limitations page, a pricing and eligibility explainer, policy hubs, case studies, and a support or escalation page.

**Visual direction:**
Stacked document forms and typographic pull quotes arranged like an editorial archive wall.

## Page 10 — Agent-See is the start of an operating discipline, not a one-time conversion

**Core insight:** The businesses that win in the prompt economy use Agent-See as a recurring operational loop that keeps discovery, trust, and execution aligned.

**Body copy:**
The businesses most likely to be found and acted on by agents are not necessarily the ones with the biggest brands. They are the ones that behave like disciplined publishers of truth and action. They maintain canonical task pages. They publish machine-readable discovery files. They align structured data with visible claims. They expose a public execution surface. They update the conversion when real business logic changes.

That is the deeper outcome of Agent-See. It does not simply generate a technical package. It gives the business a repeatable way to translate change into agent-ready infrastructure. In a web increasingly mediated by prompts, that discipline becomes a competitive advantage.

**Closing manifesto:**
Use Agent-See to convert the business. Then operate the business so models can find it, trust it, and move through it.

**Visual direction:**
A bold concluding spread with a large yellow field, black type, concentric circles, and a final manifesto-style statement anchored by the Agent-See title.

## End Notes for Magazine Adaptation

Use diagrams, callouts, and magazine-style feature typography. Keep the copy elegant and concise on-page, with supporting microcopy where needed.
